"natural" logarithm?

what makes the natural logarithm "natural"? it's not the fact that logarithms at other bases differ from natural logarithms by a constant factor. a little algebra shows that if \( e \) has this property, then so does every other base.

\[ \log_x n = \frac{\ln n}{\ln x} \implies \frac{\log_x n}{\log_x y} = \frac{\frac{\ln n}{\ln x}}{\frac{\ln y}{\ln x}} = \frac{\ln n}{\ln y} = \log_y n \]

natural logarithms are "natural" because they appear in contexts that don't explicitly invoke \( e \), or even the concept of exponentiation. the first of these is in the study of the very simple differential equation \( \frac{dx}{dt} = x \). swapping the denominator and integrating both sides gives

\[ \int_{x(0)}^{x(t)} \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{x} = \int_0^t \mathrm{d}t \implies \ln x = t \implies x = e^t \]

which is your basic exponental growth formula.

buried in there is the fact that the natural logarithm is the integral of \( \frac{1}{x} \), which to my mind gives the reason to call them "natural": they can be calculated easily by taking the area under the curve from 1 to the argument, which is approximated well (for large numbers) by a prefix of the harmonic series. this amounts to Riemann sum on the curve with partition width 1 and its difference from the true integral is bounded by the Euler-Mascheroni constant, which is a little over 0.577. the wonderful old-timey name for this operation is "quadrature of the hyperbola" and it gives another tantalizing hint about "where \( e \) comes from" that's different from the usual limit of Bernoulli's compound interest formula.
